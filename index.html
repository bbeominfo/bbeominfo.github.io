Enhancing Colon Cancer Risk Prediction with Polygenic Risk Scores

ML Developer · Georgia Institute of Technology · Bio-MIBLab
Machine LearningPRSLogistic RegressionRandom ForestUK BiobankSMOTEPlink 2.0
Summary

First-author IEEE EMBC 2025 paper integrating Polygenic Risk Scores with clinical features for 10-year colon cancer risk prediction on the UK Biobank cohort. By combining 5.7M SNP variants with non-genetic factors and addressing class imbalance via SMOTE, the model reduced false negatives from 106 to just 4 while achieving AUC 0.82.

Problem Statement

Colon cancer causes nearly 50,000 deaths annually in the US, requiring early detection. Traditional ML models using non-genetic (NG) clinical features miss many at-risk patients. This project investigated whether integrating Polygenic Risk Scores (PRSs) derived from millions of SNPs alongside NG factors could reduce false negatives and improve 10-year colon cancer risk prediction.

Methods
Data UK Biobank cohort (442,574 White British individuals); 10-year incident CC via ICD-10 C18–C18.9
6 PRSs evaluated CRC 27/63/76/103 (PGS Catalog); CC 150, CC 5M (5.7M variants) via Plink 2.0
Class imbalance SMOTE + RandomUnderSampler; Min-Max scaling
Models Logistic Regression (L2) vs Random Forest; grid search hyperparameter tuning
Key Findings
All 6 PRSs dramatically improved recall: false negatives 106 → 4
CC 5M achieved best AUC (0.82) with recall 0.99
PRS amplified age (0.75→3.29) and BMI (0.10→1.47) coefficients additive GxE interaction
Results
0.99
Class 1 recall (NG + CC 5M PRS)
0.82
AUC (NG + CC 5M PRS)

Interpretable Neural Networks Reveal Gene Interactions Underlying Autoimmune Disease Risk

ML Contributor · Georgia Institute of Technology · Bio-MIBLab
GeneDAENeural Interaction DetectionUK BiobankPRSPsoriasisPyTorchInterpretable ML
Summary

Published at ACM BCB 2025. This project developed an interpretable neural network integrating gene-level embeddings (via GeneDAE) with clinical features to predict 10-year incident psoriasis risk in the UK Biobank. Using Neural Interaction Detection across 20,301 feature pairs, the model uncovered biologically plausible GxG and GxE interactions not captured by standard PRS methods.

Problem Statement

Polygenic Risk Scores (PRSs) aggregate genetic variant effects linearly, missing gene-by-gene (GxG) and gene-by-environment (GxE) interactions critical for autoimmune disease risk. This project developed an interpretable neural network integrating gene-level embeddings with clinical features to predict 10-year incident psoriasis and discover biologically meaningful interactions that conventional PRS methods cannot capture.

Methods
Dataset UK Biobank (~500K participants); 13,120 psoriasis cases (ICD-10 L40.0–L40.9), restricted to European ancestry
GeneDAE (PRS2) Denoising autoencoder on 8,817 MHC variants (chr6); produced 159 interpretable gene-level embeddings
Feature sets NG only (49 clinical) · NG+PRS1 (univariate PRS) · NG+PRS2 (208 features); tested with LR and NN
Neural Interaction Detection (NID) 20,301 unique pairs (11,628 GxG, 7,497 GxE, 1,176 NG-NG); Bonferroni-corrected t-tests (α=0.01)
Key Findings
MIR3135B, BAK1, DXO emerged as hub genes consistent with psoriasis and smoking literature
GxG effects stronger than GxE gene interaction networks drive psoriasis susceptibility
7/10 top GxG gene pairs had prior psoriasis associations validated in literature
First systematic application of interpretable NNs with gene-level embeddings for autoimmune disease prediction
Results

Multimodal Modeling of Postprandial Glucose Response for Personalized Nutrition

ML Developer · Georgia Institute of Technology
XGBoostCGMMicrobiomeMultimodal FusionOptunaLightGBM
Summary

IEEE BHI 2025 Data Competition Award winner. This project investigated whether a meal's Carbohydrate Caloric Ratio (CCR) can be inferred from post-prandial glucose response (PPGR) recorded by CGM devices, fused with gut microbiome and clinical data. XGBoost with multimodal fusion achieved Pearson r=0.40, with gut microbiome features dominating prediction.

Problem Statement

Can a meal's Carbohydrate Caloric Ratio (CCR) be inferred from the post-prandial glucose response (PPGR) measured by CGM? This project explored whether glucose dynamics alone or fused with microbiome, anthropometric, and clinical data could enable automated dietary inference for personalized nutrition.

Methods
Dataset CGMacros: 45 participants (healthy, pre-diabetic, T2D), 10-day free-living, CGM + meal logs + microbiome
Features 6 PPGR features + early fusion of bio, gut health, microbes (1,980 species)
Models RF, GB, XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost; Optuna tuning; stacking ensemble
Key Findings
PPGR alone insufficient (r=0.11) multimodal fusion: XGBoost r=0.40
Gut microbiome (Bacteroides, Lachnospira) dominated feature importance
Lunch most predictable (r=0.80); breakfast least (r=0.60)

Integrating LLMs into a Semi-Automated Clinical Documentation Pipeline for Pediatric Cancer SCPs

ML / NLP Developer · Georgia Tech & CHOA · Bio-MIBLab
GPT-4oClinical NLPEpic EHRETL PipelinePrompt EngineeringFew-Shot Learning
Summary

IEEE TBME paper under review. A hybrid clinical documentation pipeline combining deterministic ETL and GPT-4o prompting to automate Survivorship Care Plan generation from Epic EHR data for 1,120 pediatric cancer survivors. Achieved 99.86% chemotherapy concordance, 100% CAR-T accuracy, and 97.8% HSCT detection rate.

Problem Statement

Survivorship Care Plans (SCPs) for pediatric cancer survivors are labor-intensive and error-prone. Critical treatment events (radiation, surgery, HSCT, CAR-T) are buried in unstructured clinical notes and cannot be reliably extracted by rule-based systems alone.

System Architecture
Dual-track pipeline Track 1: deterministic ETL (BSA, regex, medication mapping); Track 2: GPT-4o prompting for unstructured narratives
Surgery 4 strategies: zero-shot → definitions → few-shot → few-shot + definitions; best = 96.61% accuracy
HSCT JSON output with date normalization, copy-forward error correction, majority voting
CAR-T Strict confirmation rules; 100% accuracy despite extreme class imbalance

Interpretable Neural Networks Reveal Gene Interactions Underlying Autoimmune Disease Risk

ML Contributor · Georgia Institute of Technology · Bio-MIBLab
GeneDAENeural Interaction DetectionUK BiobankPRSPsoriasisPyTorchInterpretable ML
Summary

Developed a real-time vision-based web service for physiognomy-based facial feature classification using ML. Led both ML model design and web interface as primary ML Engineer and Designer. Received Outstanding AI Project Award from Korea Creative Content Agency (KOCCA) in 2022.

Problem Statement

Polygenic Risk Scores (PRSs) aggregate genetic variant effects linearly, missing gene-by-gene (GxG) and gene-by-environment (GxE) interactions that may be critical for autoimmune disease risk. This project developed an interpretable neural network integrating gene-level embeddings with clinical features to predict 10-year incident psoriasis and discover biologically meaningful interactions that conventional PRS methods cannot capture. To our knowledge, the first systematic application of interpretable NNs with gene-level embeddings for autoimmune disease prediction in a real-world dataset.

Methods
Dataset UK Biobank (~500K participants); 13,120 psoriasis cases (ICD-10 L40.0–L40.9), 1,184 incident within 10 years; restricted to European ancestry
GeneDAE (PRS2) Denoising autoencoder trained on 8,817 MHC variants (chr6); produced 159 interpretable gene-level embeddings
PRS1 baseline Univariate PRS via L1-regularized logistic regression on MHC variants; sigmoid-scaled
Feature sets NG only (49 clinical features) · NG+PRS1 · NG+PRS2 (208 features); each tested with Logistic Regression and Neural Network
Neural Interaction Detection (NID) Pairwise interaction scoring via first hidden layer weights; 20,301 unique pairs (11,628 GxG · 7,497 GxE · 1,176 NG-NG); Bonferroni-corrected t-tests (α=0.01)
Key Findings
NG+PRS2 achieved comparable AUC to PRS1 models while enabling far richer biological interpretability
GxG effects stronger than GxE gene interaction networks drive psoriasis susceptibility more than environment alone
MIR3135B, BAK1, DXO emerged as hub genes with strong interactions consistent with prior psoriasis and smoking literature
Known HLA-C, CDSN, PSORS1 region interactions confirmed; SBP × life stress × PRS1 three-way interaction detected
7/10 top GxG gene pairs had prior psoriasis associations validated in literature

Interpretable Gene Embedding and Variant Prioritization for Orofacial Cleft Using GeneDAE

ML Researcher · Georgia Institute of Technology · Bio-MIBLab
GeneDAEIntegrated GradientsGWASUK BiobankSparse AutoencoderNon-coding VariantsIRF6MPRA
Summary

An end-to-end workflow for complex disease genetics: starting from case-control genotype data for orofacial cleft (OFC), we use GeneDAE (a sparse denoising autoencoder) to learn interpretable gene-level representations, then apply Integrated Gradients to prioritize non-coding variants as experimentally testable hypotheses. The pipeline bridges GWAS-level signals and wet-lab validation, connecting model-derived variant rankings to functional evidence from MPRA, luciferase, and CRISPR assays in the literature.

Motivation

GWAS for complex diseases like orofacial cleft yields many variant signals, but non-coding variants are particularly difficult to interpret and prioritize for follow-up experiments. Standard PRS approaches summarize genetic risk as a single scalar, losing the gene-level resolution needed for mechanistic understanding. We built an end-to-end workflow that produces interpretable gene embeddings and a ranked shortlist of candidate variants actionable for functional validation.

Data
UK Biobank cleft cases and matched controls; SNP genotypes encoded as 0/1/2 genotype matrix
Standard QC pipeline with population structure correction; designed to integrate de novo variants from hospital cohorts if needed
GeneDAE Model
Sparse Denoising Autoencoder that learns gene embeddings directly from genotype data
Variant-to-gene connections constrained by a biologically informed adjacency mask, making embeddings interpretable at the gene level rather than operating as a black box
Each gene embedding summarizes disease-relevant genetic signal across all variants in that gene window
Gene-Level Signal Validation
Trained encoder transforms individuals into gene embedding space; embeddings tested for case-control separation at known loci
IRF6 locus used as proof-of-concept: embedding captures the direction of association consistent with GWAS-level signal, confirming the model learns biologically meaningful representations
Variant Prioritization via Integrated Gradients
Integrated Gradients applied to attribute each gene embedding back to individual input variants
Variant importance plots generated for IRF6; top-ranked variant rs2235371 matches a well-established marker in the OFC literature, providing convergent validity
Non-coding variants in top rankings become regulatory candidates for functional follow-up
Literature Integration
Layer A (historical anchor): Rahimov et al. 2008 (Nat Genet) showed rs642961 in the IRF6 enhancer disrupts a TFAP2A binding motif, providing a mechanistic story for how non-coding variants at this locus alter function
Layer B (high-throughput functional screen): 2025 Nat Commun paper used MPRA and luciferase assays to test OFC locus SNPs for allele-specific regulatory activity, with CRISPR confirmation for select hits; critically, not all well-known SNPs show allele differences in every assay, reflecting context- and condition-dependent effects
These two layers provide "connect the dots" evidence: our model narrows the search space, and existing functional data contextualizes which candidates are most experimentally tractable
Validation Pipeline
Step 1: Association signal or model-based prioritization (GeneDAE + Integrated Gradients)
Step 2: Reporter assay (MPRA / luciferase) to confirm allele-specific regulatory activity
Step 3: TF binding confirmation (EMSA, ChIP) to identify mechanistic basis
Step 4: CRISPR editing to verify effect on gene expression
Step 5: Phenotype assay to connect to functional outcome where applicable
Outputs
Gene
Interpretable gene embeddings and gene-level risk signals per individual
Variant
Integrated Gradients-based top variant shortlist for functional follow-up
Evidence
GWAS and wet-lab literature-connected candidate justification
Actionable
Candidates ready for MPRA, luciferase, TF binding, and CRISPR experiments
