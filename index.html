Enhancing Colon Cancer Risk Prediction with Polygenic Risk Scores

ML Developer · Georgia Institute of Technology · Bio-MIBLab
Machine LearningPRSLogistic RegressionRandom ForestUK BiobankSMOTEPlink 2.0
Problem Statement

Colon cancer causes nearly 50,000 deaths annually in the US, requiring early detection. Traditional ML models using non-genetic (NG) clinical features miss many at-risk patients. This project investigated whether integrating Polygenic Risk Scores (PRSs) — derived from millions of SNPs — alongside NG factors could reduce false negatives and improve 10-year colon cancer risk prediction.

Methods
Data — UK Biobank cohort (442,574 White British individuals); 10-year incident CC identified via ICD-10 codes C18–C18.9
NG Features — Age, BMI, family history, lifestyle factors (smoking, alcohol, physical activity), life stress events, medication use
6 PRSs evaluated — CRC 27, CRC 63, CRC 76, CRC 103 (PGS Catalog); CC 150, CC 5M (5.7M variants) calculated via Plink 2.0
Class imbalance — Addressed with SMOTE + RandomUnderSampler; Min-Max scaling applied to non-binary features
Models — Logistic Regression (L2, liblinear) vs. Random Forest; hyperparameter-tuned via grid search
Key Findings
LR outperformed RF — low feature correlation in NG data favored LR's independence assumptions
All 6 PRSs dramatically improved recall: false negatives reduced from 106 → 4
CC 5M (5.7M variants) achieved best AUC (0.82) with recall 0.99 — best balance of sensitivity and discrimination
PRS amplified effect of age (coefficient: 0.75 → 3.29) and BMI (0.10 → 1.47), suggesting additive interaction between genetic and lifestyle risk
Results
0.99
Class 1 recall (NG + CC 5M PRS)
0.82
AUC (NG + CC 5M PRS)
106 → 4
False negatives reduced
5.7M
SNP variants in best PRS (CC 5M)
My Contributions
Led ML model development — LR and RF pipeline with hyperparameter tuning and evaluation
Designed class imbalance strategy (SMOTE + undersampling) and feature preprocessing
Conducted PRS comparative analysis across 6 scores and interpreted feature importance results
First-authored the IEEE EMBC 2025 paper (accepted)
Future Work
Extend analysis beyond White British ancestry to reduce generalizability limitations
Balance minimization of both false positives and false negatives for clinical deployment
Explore interaction terms between PRS and lifestyle features for mechanistic insights
02 · Spring 2025 · IEEE BHI 2025 Data Competition Award

Multimodal Modeling of Postprandial Glucose Response for Personalized Nutrition

ML Developer · Georgia Institute of Technology
XGBoostCGMMicrobiomeMultimodal FusionOptunaRandom ForestLightGBM
Problem Statement

Can a meal's Carbohydrate Caloric Ratio (CCR) be inferred from the post-prandial glucose response (PPGR) measured by continuous glucose monitors (CGM)? This project explored whether glucose dynamics alone — or fused with microbiome, anthropometric, and clinical data — could enable automated dietary inference for personalized nutrition and metabolic disease prevention.

Methods
Dataset — CGMacros: 45 participants (healthy, pre-diabetic, T2D), 10-day free-living period, CGM + meal logs + microbiome profiles
PPGR Features — 6 physiologically interpretable features: baseline glucose, peak rise, time-to-peak, peak value, iAUC, return-to-baseline
Multimodal fusion — Early fusion of PPGR + bio (clinical/anthropometric) + gut health test (pathway indices) + microbes (1,980 bacterial species)
Models — RF, Gradient Boosting, XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost; each tuned with Optuna (TPE, 20 trials); stacking ensemble with Ridge meta-learner
Meal-type analysis — Stratified evaluation across breakfast, lunch, dinner, snack
Key Findings
PPGR alone insufficient: r = 0.11, NRMSE = 0.21 — glucose dynamics don't uniquely determine meal composition in free-living data
Multimodal fusion substantially improved: XGBoost achieved r = 0.40, NRMSE = 0.20
Gut microbiome dominated feature importance — Bacteroides and Lachnospira genera consistently top-ranked
Meal-type analysis: lunch (r=0.80) and dinner (r=0.77) most predictable; breakfast (r=0.60) most challenging due to overnight fasting variability

AirHealth: Interactive Visualization & Forecasting of Air Quality and Health Outcomes in the U.S.

ML Developer & Visualization Engineer · Georgia Institute of Technology
LSTMSARIMAXGRURandom ForestD3.jsEPA APINOAA APICDC WONDER
Problem Statement

Air pollution is a major driver of cardiovascular and respiratory disease, yet existing research lacks an integrated, scalable framework to quantify and visualize these relationships across time and space. This project built a nationwide, ML-powered interactive dashboard linking multi-pollutant air quality data to public health outcomes across all 50 U.S. states (2018–2030).

Data Integration
EPA AQS API — PM2.5, PM10, O3, NO2, SO2, CO at state-month level (2018–2025)
NOAA GHCND — 7 meteorological variables: wind speed, precipitation, temperature, snowfall
CDC WONDER — Monthly COPD (J40–J44) and IHD (I20–I25) mortality at state level
Urban data — FHWA vehicle miles traveled, BTS flight statistics, MODIS NDVI vegetation indices
Unified 50-state monthly panel via left joins; missing values handled with polynomial interpolation (degree 2) + forward/backward fill
Modeling Framework
Time-series forecasting — SARIMAX (per-state), LSTM, GRU; LSTM selected as final model (lowest MAE); AR-LSTM for recursive forecasting to 2030
Top features — Permutation importance identified NDVI, O3 mean/max/AQI, and temperature as most influential
Health outcome prediction — Random Forest (R² = 0.98 for IHD deaths) and Gradient Boosting outperformed Ridge baseline (R² = −0.67)
What-If simulation — OLS-based real-time calculator: users adjust PM2.5/meteorology sliders and visualize projected mortality changes

Active Learning-Guided Protein Mutation Effect Prediction

ML Developer · Georgia Institute of Technology
ESM-1vMLP EnsembleActive LearningPyTorchBioPythonMC DropoutGaussian Process
Problem Statement

Predicting the functional impact of single-point protein mutations is critical for drug development and precision medicine. This project used Deep Mutational Scanning (DMS) scores as a fitness proxy and combined protein language model embeddings with biochemical features to predict mutation effects with active learning for iterative refinement.

Methods
Feature Extraction — 1280-dim mean-pooled ESM-1v embeddings + 6 biochemical features (aromaticity, hydrophobicity, isoelectric point, helix/turn/sheet fraction) → 1286-dim input
MLP Ensemble (Final) — 4-layer MLP (1286→512→256→64→1), LayerNorm + LeakyReLU + Dropout(0.4), AdamW 3×10⁻⁴, 40 epochs, ensembled across 3 random seeds
Benchmarked — CNN (ESM-2 + RF feature selection), RNN (one-hot encoding), ESM-1v partial fine-tuning (layers 31–32)
Active Learning — 3 query strategies: MC Dropout uncertainty, ESM ΔlogP (log-probability difference), UCB-based Gaussian Process sampling
Key Findings
MLP ensemble outperformed all architectures including ESM fine-tuning
Frozen PLM + downstream MLP more effective than partial fine-tuning in low-data settings (fine-tuning: r=0.18, required 40–60GB GPU)
Full-sequence mean pooling (r=0.49) outperformed CLS token and 11-mer windowing approaches

Drug-Disease Association Modeling via Heterogeneous Graph Neural Networks

Deep Learning Developer · Georgia Institute of Technology
PyTorch GeometricGCNR-GCNHGTSAGEConvNode2VecSentence-BERTHetionetNeo4j
Problem Statement

Traditional drug discovery is lengthy and costly. This project predicted drug-disease associations using heterogeneous GNNs on the Hetionet biomedical knowledge graph, enabling drug repurposing by identifying existing compounds that may treat unknown diseases — including zero-shot generalization to unseen diseases via semantic embeddings.

Methods — 4 Architectures Benchmarked
Node2Vec + GCN — Structural baseline; biased random walk embeddings into 2-layer homogeneous GCN. Highest internal AUC (0.99) but strong overfitting.
Sentence Transformer + HeteroConv (Final) — all-MiniLM-L6-v2 384-dim embeddings into 2-layer SAGEConv with 13 meta-relations; zero-shot capable for unseen diseases
Node2Vec + R-GCN — 5-layer relational GCN with relation-specific parameters across 8 meta-edges; AUC 0.96
Node2Vec + HGT — 2-layer Heterogeneous Graph Transformer with per-node-type multi-head attention; AUC 0.77
Key Findings
Simple SAGEConv outperformed attention-based HGT in generalization to external data (HINGRL-B)
Semantic embeddings enabled zero-shot prediction for unseen diseases — key advantage over purely structural approaches
High internal AUC ≠ real-world utility; GCN showed degree centrality bias, recommending same compounds across all diseases
Results

Integrating LLMs into a Semi-Automated Clinical Documentation Pipeline for Pediatric Cancer Survivorship Care Plans

ML / NLP Developer · Georgia Institute of Technology & CHOA · Bio-MIBLab
GPT-4oClinical NLPEpic EHRETL PipelinePrompt EngineeringFew-Shot LearningRegex
Problem Statement

Survivorship Care Plans (SCPs) are essential clinical documents for pediatric cancer survivors — summarizing treatment exposures, late effects, and surveillance recommendations. Despite affecting 500,000+ survivors nationwide, SCP generation remains labor-intensive and error-prone. Critical treatment events (radiation, surgery, HSCT, CAR-T) are buried in unstructured clinical notes and cannot be reliably extracted by rule-based systems alone.

System Architecture
Dual-track pipeline — Track 1: deterministic ETL (BSA calculations, regex, medication mapping) for structured drug data; Track 2: GPT-4o LLM prompting for unstructured clinical narratives
Data sources — Epic Clarity EHR (medication orders, MAR, height/weight history), clinical notes (H&P, progress notes, discharge summaries, procedure notes), imaging history
Deterministic track — Mosteller BSA formula, medication name standardization, cumulative dose aggregation (mg/kg → mg/m²)
LLM track — Structured prompt engineering with inclusion/exclusion criteria, few-shot examples, JSON output schema; applied to CAR-T, HSCT, radiation, and surgery extraction
Prompt Engineering Strategies
CAR-T — Strict confirmation rules (explicit infusion only); counterfactual phrasing to reject eligibility/bridging/lymphodepletion mentions
HSCT — JSON output: hsct_type, source, HLA match, transplant date; post-processing: date normalization, copy-forward error correction, note-frequency majority voting
Surgery — 4 strategies compared: zero-shot → definitions → few-shot → few-shot + definitions; best = few-shot + definitions (96.61% global accuracy)
Radiation — Explicit confirmation of completed therapeutic radiation; excluded diagnostic, speculative, planned, or template-pasted mentions
Results
99.86%
Chemotherapy concordance (N=1,120 patients, 57 drugs)
100%
CAR-T detection accuracy (extreme class imbalance)
97.8%
HSCT detection; 96% exact-match on transplant dates
96.61%
Surgery extraction accuracy (few-shot + definitions)
Key Findings
Pipeline uncovered errors in manually-curated validation data — 39% of discrepancies were human abstraction errors
Few-shot + definitions: amputation F1 0.25 → 0.80; pelvic surgery F1 0.36 → 0.83
Dual-track design: deterministic logic for structured numerical computation, LLM for heterogeneous narrative semantics
My Contributions
Developed LLM prompting strategies for CAR-T, HSCT, radiation, and surgery clinical entity extraction
Implemented and evaluated the 4-strategy surgery prompting comparison
Contributed to ETL pipeline integration and post-processing logic

08 · Spring 2025 · CS 7641 Algorithms in Bioinformatics

RNA Secondary Structure Prediction: A Unified Benchmark from Dynamic Programming to Deep Learning

ML Developer · Georgia Institute of Technology
PyTorchTransformerLSTMRNNDNABERT-2NussinovArchiveIIbpRNA
Problem Statement

RNA secondary structure — the set of base-pairing interactions defining a molecule's 2D conformation — is central to gene regulation, catalysis, and protein synthesis. Predicting structure from sequence alone is a long-standing challenge. This project established a unified benchmark comparing Nussinov DP, RNN/LSTM, and Transformer-based models under identical training and evaluation conditions on the same datasets and decoding pipeline.

Methods
Formulation — Given RNA sequence X, predict pairwise matrix P ∈ [0,1]ⁿˣⁿ; decode via Nussinov DP into dot-bracket structure; evaluate with PPV, Sensitivity, F1
Datasets — ArchiveII (3,000+ validated sequences); bpRNA-NF-15.0 (15,000+, family-wise split for generalization)
Nussinov DP — Classic baseline: maximize base pairs (A–U, G–C, G–U); implemented from scratch
RNN / LSTM — GRU-based RNN and bidirectional LSTM; L×L contact matrix via dot-product; BCEWithLogitsLoss; early stopping
Transformer (scratch) — Encoder-only; learnable positional embeddings; multi-head self-attention; pairwise scoring head; weighted BCE for class imbalance
Pretrained DNABERT-2 — HuggingFace checkpoint; frozen encoder + light bilinear pairwise head; fine-tuned 2 epochs on ArchiveII
Key Findings
Nussinov: majority of sequences F1 < 0.1; fails on long-range helices and multi-loop junctions — expected given algorithmic simplicity
RNN/LSTM: high recall (~0.09–0.72) but precision near zero — evaluation pipeline issue (decoder pairs not correctly mapped to binary matrix), not model failure
Transformer (scratch): collapsed to all-negative prediction — severe class imbalance not corrected by naive BCE loss
DNABERT-2: opposite failure — over-predicted pairs (recall 0.96, precision 0.004); DNA-trained model poorly adapted to RNA pairwise task with frozen encoder
Core insight: simple BCE loss insufficient for highly imbalanced pairwise RNA labels — needs focal loss, weighted sampling, or architecture-specific solutions
Results
<0.1
Nussinov F1 for majority of sequences
0.96
DNABERT-2 recall (over-prediction failure)
0.0
Scratch Transformer F1 (all-negative collapse)
4
Model families benchmarked under identical conditions
My Contributions
Implemented Transformer from scratch — embedding, positional encoding, multi-head self-attention, pairwise scoring head
Set up and fine-tuned DNABERT-2 pretrained encoder with pairwise classification head on ArchiveII
Designed all Transformer experiments: threshold sensitivity, weighted loss, pretrained vs. scratch comparison
Diagnosed failure modes across all 4 architectures and co-authored the final report
Future Work
Focal loss or weighted BCE sampling to address severe class imbalance
RNA-specific pretrained models (RNA-FM, Uni-RNA) instead of DNA-centric DNABERT-2
MEA decoder instead of Nussinov for more accurate structure decoding
Family-wise cross-validation on bpRNA-NF-15.0 for proper generalization testing
